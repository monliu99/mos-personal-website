Last week I helped run Yale SOM's Responsible AI Conference. Mostly it was me standing in rooms with people from Google Jigsaw and OpenAI, pretending I belonged there, while everyone said things that made me feel like I was simultaneously exactly where I should be and also entirely unprepared.

The consumer protection panel was the one that got its hooks in me. They talked about users falling in love with AI systems, which is the sort of sentence that would have sounded like science fiction five years ago and now just sounds like Tuesday. Dave from Google said something about figuring out what people actually mean when they're talking to an AI — especially when they're being indirect or playful or sad in ways they don't quite understand themselves. The problems aren't theoretical anymore. They're here, walking around in the world.

What I keep thinking about is the tension between safety and utility, which is a fancy way of saying that the safest product is one nobody uses and the most useful one is probably dangerous in ways we haven't figured out yet. But preventing harm isn't the same thing as enabling good. The work isn't about stopping bad things from happening. It's about making it possible for extraordinary things to happen without everything falling apart.

Alice from Data & Society talked about cognitive off-loading, about kids losing something they haven't even identified yet. I worry about this too. But I also wonder about the kid who's failing algebra because the teacher moves too fast and the textbook assumes too much. What if an AI could explain it six different ways without getting annoyed? What if we're so busy mourning what they might lose that we forget to notice what they might gain?

Ziad mentioned that 99% of users are innocuous and positive, which is either comforting or terrifying depending on the direction you let your mind go. The question I keep turning over is how you build something safe by default without making it impossible for that 99% to do anything interesting.

The technical questions are hard. The social questions are harder. Our systems for governance move at the speed of something being pulled through mud, while the technology itself does not. The gap between them is where all the anxiety lives.

I left feeling like this matters, which is not exactly an insight but is what I have. Not because I have answers — I don't — but because the questions feel urgent and the people working on them are, for the most part, trying very hard to get it right, which is maybe all you can ask.
